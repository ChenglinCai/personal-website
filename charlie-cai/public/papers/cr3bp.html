Research and Generalization on the Planar Circular Restricted Three-Body Problem

Research and Generalization on the Planar Circular Restricted Three-Body Problem

Chenglin Cai

Abstract

The coplanar circular restricted three-body problem has important applications in the study of celestial bodies in the solar system and the design of space telescope orbits, among which the five Lagrangian equilibrium points are the most important in research and application. We first study the stability of the Lagrangian equilibrium points of the coplanar circular restricted three-body problem and determine the stability conditions and orbit adjustment strategies. An own-developed numerical integrator was used to simulate three-body motion; a state-modification algorithm was developed to station-keep the satellite in orbit (increase stability and prevent it from flying away) and to simultaneously fix errors in conserved properties; novel iterative experiments were conducted to explore the impact of the mass ratio of the two dominant bodies as well as the impact of the initial displacement and velocity perturbation of the third body on orbital stability near Lagrange points. Our simulation concludes that the stability of unstable Lagrange point orbits increases as the mass ratio increases, decreases as initial disturbance increases, and can be enhanced by effective station-keeping operations.

Then, we further study the generalization of the coplanar circular restricted three-body problem. Under the condition that the third celestial body does not affect the movement of the other two celestial bodies, the motion of the third celestial body is studied after breaking the “coplanar” and “circular” constraints by introducing the inclination angle and eccentricity of orbits. Here, we use MERCURY, a widely-used N-body numerical integrator in astrophysics to study the orbital variability of planets in binary systems, such as Pandora planet in the famous film Avatar. As the planetary orbital eccentricities and inclinations increase, the planetary orbits show faster-changing oscillation over a period of thousands of years. For these planets in binary systems, climate change is too dramatic for the development of intelligent beings. The results help us to understand the potential habitable zones for intelligent beings in the Universe.

Keywords: N-body Dynamics, Numerical Simulations, Exoplanets, Habitable Zone

Introduction

The two-body problem is a well-known physical problem that describes the periodic motion of two bodies around their center of mass and is easily solvable in elementary physics textbooks. However, adding to the system a third mass greatly complicates this model into a three-body problem [1]. The general three-body problem that describes the motion of any three objects freely in 3-dimensional space solely under the gravitational attraction by each other has no closed-form solution and is chaotic, whereas its particular case, the coplanar circular restricted three-body problem, has specific solutions [2].

The coplanar circular restricted three-body problem is described by Newtonian equations of the periodic and non-chaotic motions of three objects, two with dominant mass and one with negligible mass, in coplanar circular orbits around their center of mass [3].

In a circular restricted three-body system, there are five special positions where objects on it co-rotate in the same angular frequency with the two dominant bodies around the center of mass, which are named the Lagrange points and have labels L1, L2, L3, L4, and L5 [4]. As Figure 1.1 shows, the first three Lagrange points are collinear with the two dominant bodies, and the latter two form equilateral triangles with the two dominant bodies [5]. A Lagrange point is stable if objects can orbit stably around it, and whereas L4 and L5 are generally stable given sufficient mass-ratio between the dominant bodies, L1, L2, and L3 are not stable, which makes any third body on it tend to wander off under inevitable perturbations [6].

Figure 1.1: Simplified Diagram of Lagrange Points of the Earth-Sun System [7]

The circular restricted three-body problem is one of the most significant problems in dynamic astrophysics because of its application in orbital dynamics, chaos theory, and astrodynamic operations. The Lagrange points are of great applicative significance since their co-rotating nature enables both artificial spacecrafts and natural celestial bodies to orbit on them without additional thrust [8].

For applications in artificial spacecrafts, the Lagrange points are home to a great number of spacecrafts. L1 of the Sun-Earth system is currently the home of SOHO (Solar and Heliospheric Observatory Satellite) which provides consistent observation of the Sun [9], ACD (Advanced Composition Explorer) which analyzes high-energy particle compositions in the solar wind [10], and DSCOVR (Deep Space Climate Observatory) which takes photographs of the sunlit Earth [11]. L2 is currently the home of the Gaia probe which performs high-precision astrometry [12], Spektr-RG which conducts surveys on the galaxies [13], and the newly launched JWST (James Webb Space Telescope) which will extend the study of the formation of the early universe, the evolution of galaxies and dark matter, the early development of stars and planets, and the properties of the solar system [14].

Specifically, the JWST, after 6 months of commission to 1.5×106km1.5 \times 10^{6}\ km away from the Earth, arrives and stays around L2 in order to achieve optimal efficiency of data transmission, ease of repair, and effective shielding of sunlight in its 10.510.5-year operating lifetime [15]. However, during its operation, JWST would inevitably wander away from its path due to the inherent instability of L2 that would be magnified not only by perturbation in the solar system such as the accelerative torque and thrust generated by photon striking the sunshield with a maximum effective area 163m2163{\ m}^{2}\ but also by unpredictable events such as striking of micrometeoroids that had already occurred [15][16][17]. The modification to the correct path requires thrusters to work with annual Δv≈2.43ms\Delta v \approx 2.43\frac{m}{s} given a delta-v budget of 25.5ms25.5\frac{m}{s} [14]. With velocity-modifying station-keeping every 2121 days, the JWST will consistently be able to send inspiring data back to Earth, as exemplified by the following test Mid-Infrared Instrument image of the Large Magellanic Cloud with unprecedented resolution [14][18].

Figure 1.2: Test Image from Webb Space Telescope Mid-Infrared Instrument of the Large Magellanic Cloud in Comparison to Previous Image from Spitzer Space Telescope Infrared Array Camera [18]

For applications in natural celestial bodies, Trojan asteroids share orbits with planets around a star or a moon and co-rotates stably around the leading Lagrange point, L4, and the trailing Lagrange point, L5. For example, the clusters of asteroids at Lagrange points leading and trialing Jupiter - the Greeks and the Trojans respectively as from the Trojan War - are visualized in Figure 1.3, representing a great proportion of the more-than-70007000 Trojan asteroids in the solar system [19]. Similarly, concentrations of dust were also found at stable Lagrange points orbiting in the Earth-Moon system [23].

Figure 1.3: Visualization of Jupiter Trojan Asteroids as Evidence of Stability of Lagrange Points

Given the significance of Lagrange points, it is important to develop a detailed model to simulate the coplanar circular restricted three-body problem and to investigate stability-related processes. Past endeavors include modeling of periodicity of satellite (third body) trajectory in the rotating frame [20], the behavior of satellite under a small arbitrary constant initial disturbance [21], and specific station-keeping of the JWST [14]. As for novel contribution, this paper presents simulation in different frames, explores orbital stability as functions of perturbation and of mass ratio of dominant body which was not researched structurally before, and incorporates into the model effective error-eliminating station-keeping that applies to all circular restricted three-body problems.

Specifically, this paper will first construct and implement novel computational models in Python to simulate the coplanar circular restricted three-body problem in both rotating and static frames of reference through numerical integration and verify previous analytical results on the position of Lagrange points. Then, the deviation of the satellite from the expected orbit will be analyzed against the conservation of energy, linear momentum, and angular momentum. Next, the paper will present an updated model that not only simulates effective station-keeping for satellites but also will improve numerical precision. For the last part, the paper will obtain quantitative results on the stability of the Lagrange points and its related factors as well as discusses their mutual interactions.

After researching the coplanar circular restricted three-body problem, this paper extends the analysis by generalizing the three-body problem into elliptical and non-coplanar scenarios. Examples of this include the motion of planets in actual binary star systems. Representing a tremendous portion of all star systems in the entire universe, binary star three-body systems have attracted extensive public interest as exemplified by the planet Pandora in Avatar and the science fiction The Three-body Problem, which both allude to the habitability of planets in binary star systems. Previous study of the habitability of binary star systems primarily focuses on the water composition of the surface of the planets, but this paper points out the importance of orbital stability and variability as factors determining the habitability of planets in binary star three-body systems, whether as a potential destination of human settlement or as a possible home to extraterrestrial intelligent beings. Using the widely-used N-body numerical integrator MERCURY, the impact of eccentricity and inclination angle on the evolution of planetary orbital in generalized three-body systems are simulated, analyzed, and discussed, and our research’s implications on the habitability of exoplanets in terms of climate stability and variability are explained.

Theory for Circular Restricted Three-Body Problem

This section presents the theory of three-body motion in co-rotating frame and static frame, analytically derives the locations of Lagrange Points by solving zero-potential-gradient points in co-rotating frame, and explains theoretical predictions on third-body orbital stability and its relationship to mass ratio of the dominant bodies as well as to initial perturbation. While this section presents essential theoretical conclusions, a detailed explanation of analytical derivation is included in the Appendix.

Representation and Choice of Unit

Before theoretical derivation, the choice of unit and representation should be specifically chosen in an elegant and normalized manner to simplify computation. First, two conventional parameters are defined to be

μs=Gms\mu_{s} = Gm_{s} (1)

μe=Gme\mu_{e} = Gm_{e} (2)

where GG is the universal gravitational constant, msm_{s} is the mass of the Sun (the central and most dominant mass), and mem_{e} is the mass of Earth (the second most dominant mass). Then, for computational efficiency, it is set that

μs+μe=1\mu_{s} + \mu_{e} = 1 (3)

Finally, the Sun-Earth distance is set unit,

|ds+de|=1\left| d_{s} + d_{e} \right| = 1 (4)

where dsd_{s} and ded_{e} are the distance of the Sun and the Earth respectively to the origin.

Stability of Lagrange Points

One central topic in the study of Lagrange points in a three-body system is their stability because the normal operation of spacecrafts around them is dependent on the stability of the points. Orbital stability is defined here as the ability of the satellite to not leave an arbitrary small escape radius in a sufficiently long time given an arbitrary small initial disturbance [21].

The conventional strategy of inspecting the shape of the potential field fails here since the frame is rotating. Despite being potential maxima, L4 and L5 are unexpectedly stable since Coriolis force drives any object falling from the maxima up again. On the other hand, L1, L2, and L3, being saddle points where objects tend to fall off, are dynamically unstable as expected [7].

Therefore, to inspect orbital stability, the equations of motion of satellites around the Lagrange points are linearized, transformed into evolution matrices, and solved [21]. The eigenvalues are then obtained analytically, interpreted in the Appendix, and used to derive the ee-folding time (the time of a small perturbation to grow e-times larger) of the three unstable Lagrange points orbits:

The stability of Lagrange point orbits is dependent on the mass ratio of the two dominant bodies (mass of Sun divided by mass of Earth). For L4 and L5, the orbits should only be stable when the mass ratio satisfies the threshold below.

m1m2≥25+3692≈24.9599\frac{m_{1}}{m_{2}} \geq \frac{25 + 3\sqrt{69}}{2} \approx 24.9599 (14)

where m1m_{1} is the mass of the heavier dominant body and m2m_{2} is that of the lighter dominant body [21]. When the mass ratio falls below this threshold, the eigenvalue of L4 and L5 orbit is no longer purely imaginary (as shown in the Appendix), causing objects on L4 and L5 to unstably wander off as time progresses.

Error Analysis on Conservation Properties: Method

Computational models are inevitably subject to the accumulation of numerical errors, just as how real-world three-body systems are unavoidably disturbed by external perturbations. These two types of disturbance are indistinguishable in our simulations. To test and understand the imprecision of the model, error analysis will be performed to analyze perturbation in orbits from expectation due to computational imprecision and real-world disturbances together, allowing future simultaneous correction of both. To perform error analysis, this simulation will examine the conservation of energy, linear momentum, and angular momentum of the three-body system in the static frame.

First, a function to calculate the total kinetic energy, total gravitational potential energy, and total energy of the system will be defined. The function will be applied for every moment in the simulation to obtain the energies as functions of time. The Virial Theorem will also be verified by demonstrating how total kinetic energy and half the total potential energy sum to zero.

Effective Station-Keeping through State-Modification: Method

Given the quantifiable errors in three-body motion, station-keeping of satellites that drifted erroneously off-orbit will be effectively performed by modifying the bodies to the correct state after every time interval (or "section") with constant preset section length during the course of the simulation. To actualize this, a modifyState function will be defined in Figure 3.8.

Figure 3.8: State Modification Function to Correct Error in Three-Body Motion

Since the Sun and Earth are not subjects of concern, their state modification will be done by placing them at their original orbit with the correct velocity vector at the instant of modification. On the other hand, as shown in the Figure, the satellite will then be station-kept by undergoing the state modification that best corrects the deviation from initial energy and angular momentum. To be exact, test change of horizontal and vertical velocity in range [−2,2]\lbrack - 2,\ 2\rbrack will be performed, and the Δv\mathrm{\Delta}v vector eventually chosen will be the one that makes conserved properties of the modified state have the least absolute deviation from their initial values.

Orbital Stability Simulation over Time: Method

Next, tests will be performed to examine orbital stability over time. To be specific, drift radius error (deviation of the satellite from the correct radius of trajectory from center of mass) will be simulated as a function of time under small initial disturbances. The simulation result can also be used to check the time needed for an arbitrary pre-set deviation from the expected radius to increase by a factor of ee (ee-folding time) and to examine how stability (manifested as the quickness of deviation from the expected path) changes as mass ratio and initial disturbances change in different Lagrange points.

Orbital Stability vs. Mass Ratio

The next set of experiments will examine how the mass ratio of the two dominant bodies impacts the stability of the five Lagrange point orbits. This can be regarded as an extensional study of the circular restricted three-body problem to mass ratios that are greatly less than the celestial values, which can provide further reference to the problem. Orbital Stability vs. Initial Disturbance

The final set of experiments will examine the impact of initial disturbance on the time for the satellite to escape a certain escape radius from the expected orbit, in this case being that with an escape radius of 0.010.01 (a great value considering the normalized units). The two types of initial disturbance examined are displacement (xx) and velocity (uu) disturbance on the positive x-direction. The ranges of independent variable, x_dis_list and u_dis_list, are respectively [0.0001,0.009]\lbrack 0.0001,\ 0.009\rbrack and [0.0,0.1]\lbrack 0.0,\ 0.1\rbrack. The dependent variable, escape time, will be measured by for-iteration that breaks when the satellite’s radius is greater than the escape radius. Note that only initial disturbances on the positive xx-direction and uu-direction are considered because the thruster can only be placed on the side of the sunshield due to temperature requirements, which means any escaping in the direction opposite to the Sun will be irreparable [22].

Discussion on the Stability of Lagrange points

This section discusses the results from Section 4, links the output of each simulation together, and provides further exploration of the topic.

The coordinates of Lagrange points are verifiable in the rotating-frame simulation. Given no initial disturbance, a satellite can stay on L3, L4, and L5 virtually indefinitely (at least for 10001000 Time Units or more than 400400 years). However, satellite tends to wander off L1 and L2 despite no initial disturbance due to computational imprecision that is magnified by the unstable nature of their orbits, and the trajectory of the satellite would be visible in the rotating frame usually after 0.40.4 time units - the ee-folding time of L1 and L2.

Such numerical imprecision and disturbance accumulation are manifested by the non-conservation of linear and angular momentum during the course of the simulation, which can be corrected by the state modification algorithm in Section 3.4. Given a relatively short period of time considered (such as tens of time units), state modification every 5050 steps can effectively keep satellites in their orbits, and state modification every 1010 steps as in Section 4.4 can achieve near-perfect station-keeping and error elimination.

Given an initial disturbance, all satellites around unstable Lagrange points tend to drift away from their orbit as time elapses, with ee-folding time close to theoretical prediction. However, due to the binding nature of the orbit, if the initial disturbance is not sufficient, the satellite periodically re-approaches the original orbit. For example, for the case of L3 with 0.000010.00001 initial disturbance in Figure 5.1, the satellite first drifts far away from the orbit but eventually returns after almost 30003000 time units (~12791279 years).

Figure 5.1: Drift from Expected Radius vs. Time (L3 Orbit, Actual Mass Ratio)

To effectively tackle such accumulation of disturbance, the station-keeping algorithm can be applied, increasing the ee-folding time under the same initial disturbance for every mass ratio, as shown in Figure 5.2.

Generalization of Coplanar Circular Restricted Three-body Problem: Simulations on the Planetary Orbits in Binary Systems

After accounting for the coplanar circular restricted three-body problem, it is natural to remove some constraints so as to generalize our study. Actual binary star systems have two stars that correspond to the two dominant bodies and also planets whose mass is negligible and each one of which can be regarded as the third body. In most such a system, the two stars form a binary orbit, and the third body usually orbit around a central body, typically being the dominant body with greater mass. After the constraints of circularity and coplanarity are removed, we are interested in the evolution of the orbit of the third body, which we referentially named Pandora.

To study such generalized motion, we use the widely-used astrophysical numerical integrator MERCURY which is based on the Fortran algorithm. Our simulation is set to progress with a time step of 0.10.1 days and an accuracy parameter of 10−1410^{- 14}. Our simulation accounts for collision with the radius of the central body being 5×10−4AU5 \times 10^{- 4}\ AU and accounts for escapes with an ejection radius of 103AU.10^{3}\ AU. The output units are set in years. Iteration is expressed in asteroidal coordinates and we primarily focus on the planetary orbit’s semi-major axis, orbital eccentricity, and inclination angle with respect to binary orbit.

From “Circular” to “Elliptical”

The initial eccentricity of the binary orbit is increased from 0.00.0 to 0.80.8 with a step size of 0.20.2. The initial inclination angle between the binary orbit is controlled to be zero. The mass ratio between the two dominant bodies is controlled to be 100100. The semi-major axis ratio between the binary orbit and the planetary orbit is controlled to be 1010.

Figure 6.1: Eccentricity of Planetary Orbit Induced from Eccentricity of Binary Orbit in 2739 years

When the binary orbit is set with an initial eccentricity, a non-zero eccentricity is induced in the planetary orbit due to a change in the direction of the gravitational attractions. The eccentricity in the planetary orbit indeed originated from the non-zero binary eccentricity because when the latter is zero, the planet still orbits circularly as time progresses (a horizontal line). In a moderate range of binary eccentricity, the planetary eccentricity evolves periodically. As the binary eccentricity increases, the eccentricity of the planetary orbit is induced more drastically (higher peak value and shorter period). Finally, when the binary system exhibits a more-than-moderate eccentricity, the induced planetary eccentricity becomes quasi-periodic and irregular due to large fluctuations in gravitational attraction.

Figure 6.2: Fluctuation in Planetary Semi-major Axis due to Excessive Eccentricity in 2739 years

For all eccentricities, the inclination angle between the binary and the planetary orbit remains to be zero, which is trivial because the system is stable in the normal direction of the coplanar surface. However, whereas the time-evolution of the planetary semi-major axis is a horizontal line for binary eccentricity up to 0.60.6, deviation and fluctuation emerged as binary eccentricity boosted to 0.80.8, as shown in Figure 6.2. This shows that excessive eccentricity can provide enough imbalance of gravitational attraction so as to disturb the stability of planetary orbit.

When the ratio of the binary semi-major axis and the planetary semi-major axis is decreased, the disturbance and induction from binary eccentricity are magnified. Given a binary eccentricity of 0.250.25, when the semi-major axis ratio is decreased to 22, the planetary orbit first displays an irregularly fluctuating quasi-periodicity and collapses due to collision after around 14001400 years of running. This is because the lower semi-major axis ratio represents more frequent and complex encounter scenarios to both dominant bodies, which makes the systems to be more sensitive and unstable, as shown in Figure 6.3.

Finally, systems with both initial binary eccentricity and initial inclination angle are studied. The mass ratio and semi-major axis ratio are still controlled to be 1010 and 100100. To understand this, we can draw an analogy on the formality of Kozai resonance, in which asteroids, under the simultaneous effect of Jupiter and the Sun, show no secular evolution of semi-major axis but have the quantity

For Kozai resonance this reflects specifically the conservation of the third Delaunay momentum, but for our case, it reflects the general idea of momentum conservation that can be used to understand the relationship between planetary eccentricity and inclination. Discussion on Habitable Planets in Binary Systems

In search and discussion of the potential settlement of human civilization and the existence of intelligent beings, the stability of climate and temperature conditions is a crucial factor. To be specific, the existence of liquid water is fundamental to the habitability of exoplanets, but considering that the universe has a large amount of water molecules such as in comets that could be brought to exoplanets through collision and that planets live long enough to wait for bringing of water molecules, it is the preservation instead of origin of liquid water that is crucial. This requires the temperature of the planet to be stable in an interval that would neither boil water into water vapor that might escape through the atmosphere nor condense water into ice that is not useful for the production and reproduction of lives.

Just as the evolution of eccentricity of Earth's orbit and obliquity in the solar system drives Ice Ages and Interglacial Periods in Milanković Cycles, the average surface temperature of exoplanets is a function of semi-major axis, eccentricity, and angle of inclination of planetary orbit. Therefore, greater fluctuation in such parameters would lead to greater instability in climate and temperature.

Most binary systems exhibit non-zero eccentricity and angle of inclination, whose variations are mutually inducing. This implies that most binary systems cannot support a stable and habitable climate. To verify and exemplify this, we obtained relevant orbital data from exoplanet catalogs of binary star systems and utilized stellar temperature and radius data from NASA Exoplanet Archive to estimate the maximum equilibrium average surface temperature TeqT_{eq}\ of the exoplanets from the following equation, which is rearranged from the equality between the incoming black body radiation from the central star and the emitted black body radiation from the planet, taking reflection into account as well [25] [26].

Teq=TRa(1−Ab)14T_{eq} = T\sqrt{\frac{R}{a}}\left( 1 - A_{b} \right)^{\frac{1}{4}} (16)

Here TT\ and RR are the surface temperature and radius of the central star, aa is the planetary semi-major axis, and AbA_{b} is the planet’s albedo. More than a hundred binary systems are analyzed, and, among those with valid eccentricity data, HD 20782 b and HD 125612 b stand out to have TeqT_{eq} of 355.09K355.09\ K and 352.06K352.06\ K. However, for HD 20782 b, the high planetary orbit eccentricity (0.97) and low dominant body mass ratio (1.19) induce irregular fluctuation in the semi-major axis, as shown in Figure 6.9. On the other hand, HD 125612 b shows a more regular periodic semi-major axis fluctuation, but still varies in an approximately-30%-of-baseline range, as shown in Figure 6.10.

Figure 6.9: Evolution of Orbit of HD 20782 b

Figure 6.10: Evolution of Orbit of HD 125612 b

However, for these two cases, the planetary eccentricity and inclination are constant over time even under a 60˚ initial inclination angle. These invariabilities are presumably due to the extremely high semi-major axis ratios that are up to 10,00010,000. Nevertheless, the great variability in the planetary semi-major axis as in these two cases, and the great variability in planetary eccentricity and inclination angle as in other cases still makes most binary system exoplanets have unstable climates and be inhabitable. In the search for habitable exoplanets in binary systems, one should look for those with low binary and planetary eccentricity and inclination angle but with a high mass ratio and semi-major axis ratio.

Conclusion

The circular restricted three-body problem describes a dynamic astrophysical scenario that is crucial in areas from the operation of artificial spacecrafts to the clustering of celestial bodies, and its generalization provides essential insights into the variability and habitability of exoplanets in binary star systems. Based on theories of coordinates and stabilities of Lagrange points as well as equations of motion from Newtonian mechanics, this paper first constructs an original numerical integrator to simulate circular restricted three-body motion in both rotating and static frames and calculate conserved properties and drift radius to analyze errors and evolutions over time. Then, a state-modification algorithm is developed, achieving modeling of effective station-keeping. Next, Lagrange orbital stabilities are examined as a function of the mass ratio of dominant bodies and as a function of initial perturbations in an unstable direction, yielding a positive relationship in the former and a negative relationship in the latter and providing novel results. Finally, MERCURY is used for the generalization of the three-body problem into non-circular and non-coplanar systems, with mutual interaction and evolution of semi-major axis, eccentricity, and angle of inclination studied. It is concluded that habitable exoplanets are more likely to be found in binary systems with low binary and planetary orbital eccentricity and inclination but with high semi-major axis ratio and dominant body mass ratio. Future work could continue to focus on the generalized N-body secular evolution in the solar system and simulate the balance of delta-v consumption and orbital stability.

A.3 Equation of Motion in Static Frame

The acceleration in the xx and yy-direction in the static frame is simply the resolution of the gravitational acceleration on the axes. There's a phase difference of π\pi between the angle of the position and the angle of the acceleration to the xx-axis.

A.4 Stability Analysis

The eigenvalue of the linearized evolution matrix that describes the motion of satellites near each Lagrange point is solved [21].

For L1 and L2, there is a positive real root,

λ+=ω1+27\lambda_{+} = \omega\sqrt{1 + 2\sqrt{7}} (A22)

which suggests that L1 is unstable and any small perturbation will grow with an ee-folding time of 1λ+\frac{1}{\lambda_{+}}, which for the solar system is approximately 2323 days [6].

For L3, there’s also a positive real root,

λ+=ω3ms8me\lambda_{+} = \omega\sqrt{\frac{3m_{s}}{8m_{e}}} (A23)

which, in the context of the solar system, gives an ee-folding time of approximately 150150 years [6].

Finally, for L4 and L5, the eigenvalues are,

λ±=±iω22−27κ2−23\lambda_{\pm} = \pm i\frac{\omega}{2}\sqrt{2 - \sqrt{27\kappa^{2} - 23}} (A24)

σ±=±iω22+27κ2−23\sigma_{\pm} = \pm i\frac{\omega}{2}\sqrt{2 + \sqrt{27\kappa^{2} - 23}} (A25)

where κ=ms−mems+me\kappa = \frac{m_{s} - m_{e}}{m_{s} + m_{e}}. L4 and L5 are stable if all roots are purely imaginary. First, this requires 27κ2−23≤2\sqrt{27\kappa^{2} - 23} \leq 2. Since |κ|<1|\kappa| < 1, this condition is always satisfied. Second, this requires 27κ2−23≥027\kappa^{2} - 23 \geq 0, which gives our constraining threshold mass ratio,

m1m2≥25+3692≈24.9599\frac{m_{1}}{m_{2}} \geq \frac{25 + 3\sqrt{69}}{2} \approx 24.9599 (A26)

below which L4 and L5 become unstable Lagrange points [21].
